klonos (May 09 2020 at 02:08):

For the past couple of weeks I've been reading articles about the upcoming release of Drupal 9 (anything that comes up after googling "Drupal 9 what's new") and everyone seems to try to get people excited about the new release (organizing release parties globally etc.) ...well, I can't help but think that it is just a hoax or a marketing gimmick ...unless you are a developer, this release simply does not add anything of substantial value.

Many of these articles try to "invent" advantages/features :sweat_smile: ...they all seem to basically say:

...there are a few advantages of upgrading to Drupal 9:
1. Easier upgrade from Drupal 8
2. Contributed modules compatibility
3. You don’t have much time!

Point 1 and 2 are the same (IMO one is basically just a side-effect of the other), because nothing has really changed. They both could be summed up in "We just did code clean-up, so nothing major to break backwards compatibility". ...and point 3, no matter how these articles choose to phrase it, it's just a joke; it just ain't a feature/advantage.

I have seen the performance tests of Symfony 3.x vs 4.x and there is indeed performance gain; same as moving from php5 to php7 - no doubt. But has anyone done performance benchmark of Drupal 8.x vs 9.x? I haven't seen any article about that. ...I am really curious to see what the savings will be in the GovCMS platform after all those ~400 sites have been upgraded to GovCMS9 :thinking: ...if there is substantial difference there, then I will be impressed.

If you are a site owner, the gist of it is that you have to pay $$$ for the mere fact that D8 will become EoL; and the irony of it is that the end product itself does not have any need for that; you'll get no "tangible" benefit in the admin UI, so nothing there for content editors and site builders (and don't anyone dare mention the new Claro theme as an argument here - it is indeed a big improvement in many aspects, such as consistency and accessibility; but it is a Drupal 8 theme; it has been developed as such throughout its dev cycle. ...it just becomes the default theme in D9 - people do not have to upgrade to D9 to get it; they can have it now with D8 if they want) ...anyway, it feels that with D9 it's just that the underlying technologies that were added as dependencies are becoming EoL (symphony basically) so the end product has no option but to follow the release cycle of these technologies :shrug:

...site builders (people that use their CMS as a lego-style site building tool and only touch code if they absolutely must) also get no benefit out of D9 the way I see it; same features as Drupal 8.9.0 really.

Bottom line is that I see Drupal 9 as a developer-focused release, with code/framework improvements. Which makes me wonder why the hell Dries is calling for a community survey? The results in 2016 clearly showed what the community wanted to focus on, and the people that drive the product did the complete opposite:

klonos (Mar 08 2021 at 02:15):

@Phil Ward automated merging of changes can potentially be very dangerous @Phil Ward - code changes simply need human :eyes: ...what could help would be:

  1. larger community ...unfortunately, that in Drupal-land appears to be shrinking, and my personal interpretation of the main reason for that is the drive for a more "elite" group of developers. I also think that the idea to "get off the island" has basically not worked as they'd expected - it in fact seems to have backfired on Drupal: they were aiming to attract more "professional" developers from the larger php community, but what happened is that instead of a dev community loyal to Drupal, they got people that are excited by whatever new technology seems to be coming out every once in a while. If Drupal doesn't adopt these technologies (or adapt in ways to accomodate for them), these developers simply leave Drupal for other platforms/frameworks ...and what you get is community shrinkage over time. What they basically ended up doing is to chase the "amateur" developers that loved Drupal away ...they simply did not appreciate how hard it is to build numbers in any community :shrug: Drupal should have tried to figure out ways to retain the existing community and keep them happy, while at the same time support technologies/ways to attract new/professional "manpower" ...that could have for example been by keeping both D7 and what evolved to be D8/9/10 as two separate products, with parallel support, and allowing people to jump from the "less professional" or "legacy CMS" to the more advanced/enterprise DXP
  2. shared responsibility of project maintenance: I think that ideas like the Bug Squad and easier access to all contrib authors in Backdrop work towards that goal. Not saying that this is perfect - we definitely need more tweaking in process/permissions and how we regulate things, but the bottom line is that these methods of maintaining projects without "locking" them to only one or two maintainers, and making these few people feel as if they are "lifting the weight of the world" are good steps towards that direction.
  3. simplification of the code contribution: Drupal have only recently (this past year I believe) changed to support pull requests - it was 2020 and people still had to work with .patch files in the era of Git :rolling_eyes: ...and that could have been sorted a decade earlier perhaps (if they focused on that instead of refactoring the codebase to support composer/symfony etc.)
  4. simplification of code testing: basically https://www.drupal.org/docs/develop/git/using-git-to-contribute-to-drupal/using-live-previews-on-drupal-core-and-contrib
