Phil Ward (Nov 22 2020 at 22:06):

@Sarvasri I think a lot of folks feel the way you do about having time, but not money.

However, I would like to point out that there are also a lot of people (businesses/organizations) that have money, but don't have time or knowledge.

For this reason, I am advocating for a way to allow those who want to provide financial assistance for core, contribs or even custom, the ability to give back.

If the only way a person, organization or business can give back is through financial assistance, but there is no way for them to do so, they don't end up giving back at all. This hurts the community.

Let's take Ubercart as an example because that's what I use. I would gladly pay for additional features or support on that module because it benefits me. However, I wouldn't necessarily give back to core because that's not where my problem is. I'm left with hiring a third party developer to fix my problem and never give that back to the community because a) the maintainer doesn't want to mess with adding the fixes or b) I don't have time to add it as a contrib and then try to maintain it.

I strongly believe it hurts the community more than it helps and have observed this over the last 13+ years with Drupal.

Phil Ward (Nov 22 2020 at 22:14):

Just so we're clear, I'm not advocating for a subscription model, or commercial model (though yes, I have suggested those options and they are widely not popular for good reason).

The option I think a lot of folks can get on board with is a "donation" model where the ability to give back is available, but completely optional.

There are a LOT of things to work out, but I think it is the most forgiving to keeping the entire platform and codebase free, but still opening an avenue for a lot of the community to give back who would otherwise never give back.

I know, I shouldn't,... but can't resist to share some general thoughts about Open Source...

In the Open Source ecosystem it's not uncommon that maintainers (or engaged contributors) leave a project. There are many possible reasons for that - change of interest, lack of time...

A proprietary (closed source) software project would certainly die with that, but not necessarily an Open Source project. Why? Because the source code is available, and anyone can fork it. If there's an interested community, someone with sufficient skills can take over. I saw this happen many times.

So my conclusion: it's mainly the availability of source code, that can guarantee the continuation of a project. Given that enough people are interested in the project. Payment can be nice, but is secondary IMO.
